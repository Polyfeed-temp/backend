Learner-Centered Feedback

Learner-centered feedback is a crucial approach focused on addressing the specific needs of individual students, thereby enhancing the learning process. This approach is structured around three key dimensions: Future Impact, Sensemaking, and Agency.

Future Impact

Guiding Future Work: Feedback that helps students improve their future assignments and align their efforts with the learning goals.
Example: "Make sure to include at least one citation for each question, and remember to both cite in-line and reference correctly."

Reaching Learning Goals: Feedback that helps students achieve the desired outcomes for their course.
Example: "In your next assignment, try using more efficient algorithms instead of brute-force methods."

Developing Skills: Feedback that supports students in building learning strategies and skills that will be useful throughout their academic journey and beyond.
Example: "Please make sure to spell check your work before submission."

Sensemaking

Evaluating Strengths and Weaknesses: Feedback that clearly outlines what students did well and where they can improve.
Strength Example: "Your introduction clearly outlines the main objectives of the report, making it easy to follow."
Weakness Example: "Your referencing is not up to APA standards."

Summarizing Performance: Feedback that provides an overall view of the student’s achievements and areas for improvement in relation to the learning objectives.
Strength Example: "Your analysis of the case study was thorough and showed a strong understanding of the key concepts."
Weakness Example: "This report lacks the level of detail and description expected from a master's student."

Agency

Encouraging Active Learning: Feedback that motivates students to take an active role in their learning, such as discussing their work with the teacher or exploring additional resources.
Example: "I recommend looking up 'Gibbs cycle of reflection' and trying to apply that to your diaries."

Acknowledging Accomplishments: Feedback that recognizes the student’s achievements and encourages them for future tasks.
Example: "Great job on submitting your first reflective diary for the semester!"

Building Teacher-Student Relationships: Feedback that helps strengthen the connection between the teacher and the student.
Example: "It was a pleasure to see how you've applied the feedback from the last assignment so effectively."

Feedback Model

This model leverages the BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) architecture, a cutting-edge deep learning model renowned for its capability to understand the context and subtle nuances of text. The BERT model is employed to automate the classification of feedback according to the learner-centered feedback framework.
